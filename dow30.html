The Dow and The Dhamma

Nina van Gorkom, in her 2009 "Abhidhamma in Daily Life" book, said that the dhamma is everything that is real:

Seeing is a dhamma, it is real. Colour is a dhamma, it is real. Feeling is a dhamma, it is real.

However, it is really important to distinguish between the dhamma (lowercase) and the Dhamma (capitalized). The Dhamma, she rightly explains, is the Buddha's teaching. In particular, the higher teachings on reality (Abhidhamma) and how to use that knowledge of reality to put an end to suffering (i.e. saṃsāra).

There are three levels of the Dhamma, and thus three levels of mitzvah (or "three bases of meritorious activity" in Bhikkhu Bodhi, parlance).

[Not that it matters, as he doesn't attempt to connect the traditions, but Bhikkhu Bodhi is not only Buddhist, but also Jewish just like Mel.]

The first level of the Buddha's teachings are his teachings on the benefits of giving dāna. Level two Dhamma is comprised of teachings on ethics and morality and their return on investment.

If you smash Dhamma levels one and two together, you get a double whammy on doing good deeds. If you want to take Buddhist mitzvah to its zenith, throw in samādhi (the Pāḷi word for a focused mind) and viola: triple whammy mitzvah. That's the ultimate mitzvah.

But let's keep it simple and focus on level one. Take it step-by-step. Work hard. Don't gamble. Trade actively. Invest in the Theravādin Saṅgha.

That's the Mitzvah Capital modus operandi... Stare at heatmaps of the components of the Dow Jones Industrial Average Index or the NASDAQ 100, reflect on the economy, don't gamble, but buy low, sell high, and then hand the profits over to Theravādin monks in the form of dāna.

If that sounds like fun to you, holler!
